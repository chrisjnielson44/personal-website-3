Retrieval-augmented generation and knowledge graphs are usually described as separate patterns. One comes from the recent wave of LLM applications, the other from a longer history of symbolic AI, search, data integration, and enterprise knowledge management. Because they come from different traditions, teams sometimes talk about them like they are competing answers to the same question.

That framing is not very useful. RAG and knowledge graphs solve different problems. RAG is a way to bring relevant external context into a model's working memory. A knowledge graph is a way to represent entities and relationships in a domain. RAG helps a model find the paragraph that matters. A graph helps a system understand how things are connected.

The interesting work starts when you stop choosing between them and start asking what kind of knowledge the system needs.

## The shape of the question matters

The easiest way to think about the difference is to look at the shape of user questions.

Some questions are document-shaped. If someone asks, "What does our model risk policy say about annual validation?" the answer probably lives in a few paragraphs. The system needs to find the right policy document, retrieve the relevant section, and ask the LLM to summarize or explain it. RAG is a natural fit. The source of truth is text, and the answer can be grounded in text.

Other questions are relationship-shaped. If someone asks, "Which reports would be affected if this metric definition changed?" the answer does not live in one paragraph. It lives in a network of dependencies: metrics, dashboards, source tables, owners, downstream reports, business rules, and maybe controls. Vector search might find documents that mention the metric, but it does not naturally know the dependency chain. It can retrieve hints. It cannot reliably traverse the business.

That distinction matters because LLMs are very good at explaining context but much less reliable when they have to infer hidden structure. If the structure is important, it should not be left implicit in a chunk of text. It should be represented directly.

## What RAG gives you

RAG became popular because it solves a very real problem: models do not know your private data, and their built-in knowledge gets stale. Rather than retrain a model every time a policy changes or a document is added, you retrieve relevant content at request time and provide that content to the model.

This is powerful because most organizations already have a mountain of unstructured or semi-structured knowledge. Policies, procedures, design docs, architecture decision records, tickets, incident reports, PDFs, meeting notes, regulatory text, source code comments, and wiki pages all contain knowledge that people use every day. RAG gives an LLM a way to work with that material.

A simple RAG system has a familiar flow. Documents are split into chunks, chunks are embedded into vectors, and user queries are embedded into the same space. At runtime, the system retrieves chunks that are close to the query and places them in the model context. The model then answers using those passages.

The best version of this pattern is not just "stuff text into the prompt." Production RAG requires careful chunking, metadata, permissions, freshness checks, retrieval evaluation, reranking, and answer validation. The hard part is rarely the first demo. The hard part is making sure the system retrieves the right thing when the wording changes, the corpus grows, or the user asks a question with ambiguous terminology.

Still, RAG is a good starting point when the knowledge is textual and the main job is to explain, summarize, compare, or cite. If you want a policy copilot, documentation assistant, contract Q&A tool, research assistant, or internal search experience, RAG is often the shortest path to something useful.

## Where RAG starts to bend

RAG becomes less satisfying when relevance is not enough.

Imagine a risk analyst asks, "Why did exposure for this counterparty move between yesterday and today?" A RAG system may retrieve a few documents about exposure, counterparty risk, and daily reporting. Those passages may be relevant, but the actual answer may require knowing which trades changed, which market data shifted, which model version ran, which legal entity hierarchy applies, and which report consumes the calculated value.

The problem is not that the model needs more text. The problem is that the system needs a representation of the domain. It needs to know that a counterparty belongs to a legal entity, that a legal entity rolls up to a parent, that an exposure metric is produced by a model, that the model consumes market data, and that the result appears in a specific report. Those relationships are not incidental. They are the thing being queried.

This is where many RAG systems quietly turn into piles of heuristics. Teams add better prompts, more chunks, broader retrieval, manually curated context, and rerankers. Those improvements help, but they do not change the underlying shape of the system. If the question is relational, the system needs relational knowledge.

## What knowledge graphs give you

A knowledge graph makes relationships first-class. Instead of representing knowledge only as text, it represents entities and edges: applications depend on datasets, reports use metrics, metrics are defined by rules, rules are owned by teams, teams operate systems, systems produce outputs, and outputs feed decisions.

This sounds abstract until you put it next to a real workflow. In a large organization, people constantly ask relationship questions:

Which systems consume this dataset? Which models depend on this feature? Which controls apply to this process? Which reports use this metric? Which business units are affected by a change to this source table? Which policies mention this obligation, and which applications implement it?

These questions are hard for pure vector retrieval because the answer is not a paragraph. It is a path. Sometimes it is several paths. A graph gives the system a way to follow those paths intentionally.

Knowledge graphs also make ambiguity easier to manage. A term like "exposure" might mean different things in credit risk, market risk, treasury, or reporting. A vector database can retrieve content related to all of those meanings. A graph can attach "exposure" to specific metric definitions, owners, calculation methods, and consuming reports. The graph can help resolve which concept the user means before retrieval even happens.

The tradeoff is effort. A useful graph does not appear automatically because someone installed a graph database. You have to define the ontology, decide which entities matter, maintain relationships, handle ownership, and keep the graph fresh. A stale knowledge graph is not intelligence. It is decorative complexity.

## The real comparison

The cleanest distinction is this: RAG retrieves evidence, while a graph represents structure.

Evidence is what the model can quote, summarize, or ground itself in. Structure is what the system can traverse, constrain, and validate against. If a user asks, "What does this document say?" evidence is enough. If a user asks, "How does this change affect the business?" structure becomes necessary.

The same domain may need both. A regulatory policy is text, so RAG helps retrieve the relevant obligation. But the obligation may apply to specific controls, systems, processes, owners, and reports, so a graph helps map impact. The document explains the rule. The graph explains where the rule lands.

This is especially important in enterprise AI, where correctness is not only about producing a fluent answer. It is about lineage, permissions, ownership, auditability, and operational consequences. A model that gives a confident summary without showing the dependency path may be impressive, but it is not always useful enough for production.

## The risk point of view

Risk is a good domain for seeing why the distinction matters. Risk teams rarely ask questions that are purely textual or purely numerical. They ask questions that sit between policy, data, models, reports, controls, and business judgment.

Consider a question like, "Why did potential future exposure increase for this counterparty?" A useful answer may need the definition of PFE, the model version that produced it, recent market data movement, the portfolio of trades behind the number, the legal entity hierarchy, collateral treatment, reporting thresholds, and whether any known data quality issues were active during the run. Some of that context lives in documents. Some lives in databases. Some lives in model metadata. Some lives in lineage. Some lives in the relationship between all of those things.

RAG can retrieve the model methodology document, the reporting procedure, or the incident note from last week's run. That is valuable. But a risk analyst also needs to know which upstream datasets fed the calculation, which downstream reports consumed the value, who owns the metric, and whether the change affects a control or escalation threshold. That is graph-shaped context.

This is also where auditability changes the architecture. In a risk setting, the answer should not only sound reasonable. It should be inspectable. If an AI system says a dashboard was affected by a metric change, it should be able to show the path from metric to derived metric to report. If it cites a policy, it should show the source text. If it uses a model output, it should identify the run, version, and timestamp. The hybrid approach is not just a quality improvement. It is a way to make the AI system accountable enough for regulated work.

## The hybrid pattern

A hybrid RAG and knowledge graph system starts by treating the user question as both a language problem and a domain problem.

Suppose someone asks, "Which credit risk reports are affected by changes to Exposure at Default?" A pure RAG system might search for "Exposure at Default" and "credit risk reports." It may retrieve a metric definition, a reporting procedure, and a few dashboard docs. That is helpful, but it may miss reports that depend on derived metrics or downstream dashboards that never mention the original term.

A hybrid system can do something richer. First, it identifies "Exposure at Default" as a metric entity. Then it traverses graph relationships from that metric to derived metrics, dashboards, source tables, reports, owners, and controls. At the same time, it retrieves text describing the metric, reporting policy, and recent release notes. The LLM receives both the graph path and the textual evidence.

The context packet might include a concise graph summary:

```txt
Exposure at Default
  -> used by Expected Loss
  -> appears in Credit Portfolio Dashboard
  -> feeds Risk Committee Report
  -> owned by Credit Risk Analytics
```

It might also include document excerpts explaining how Exposure at Default is calculated and where it appears in reporting procedures. The final answer can then say not only what the metric means, but which downstream artifacts are affected and why.

That is the key: the graph does not replace RAG. It makes RAG more targeted and more explainable. The vector retriever brings in human-readable evidence. The graph brings in relationship-aware context. The model turns both into an answer a person can use.

## How I would build it

I would start small. Pick a domain where relationships are valuable and the source material already exists. Risk reporting, data lineage, model inventory, application dependencies, and metric catalogs are good candidates because the entities are durable and the relationships matter.

The first version does not need to model the whole company. It needs enough structure to answer a real class of questions better than RAG alone. Define a handful of entity types, such as metric, report, dataset, model, owner, control, and policy. Then define the important relationships: uses, produces, owns, governs, depends on, appears in, and replaces.

Once the graph exists, pair it with retrieval. Use the graph to resolve entities and expand the query. Use vector search to retrieve supporting documentation. Use permissions before either result reaches the model. Then ask the model to answer from a structured bundle that separates graph facts from document excerpts.

That separation matters. The model should know when a statement came from a governed relationship and when it came from an unstructured source. It should be able to cite documents and show graph paths. Users trust answers more when they can see where the reasoning came from.

## When not to use a graph

Not every RAG system needs a knowledge graph. If your domain is mostly unstructured text and the questions are local to documents, building a graph too early can slow you down. A support assistant that answers from product documentation may not need graph traversal. A research assistant over papers may get plenty of value from high-quality retrieval, reranking, and citation handling.

Graphs become worth the cost when the relationships have operational value outside the LLM. If the graph can also power lineage, governance, search, impact analysis, dashboards, and automation, the investment is easier to justify. If it only exists to decorate a chatbot, it may become another system to maintain without a clear payoff.

There is also a modeling risk. A badly designed ontology can force the business into categories that do not fit. The graph should emerge from real workflows, not from an abstract desire to make everything semantic.

## The decision I use

When I am thinking about RAG versus knowledge graphs, I ask one practical question: what would a human need to answer this well?

If a human would search a few documents and quote the relevant passages, RAG is probably enough. If a human would open a lineage tool, check ownership, trace dependencies, inspect a metric catalog, and then read supporting documentation, the AI system probably needs a graph and RAG together.

This is why hybrid systems feel inevitable in serious enterprise settings. Text explains the world, but relationships organize it. RAG gives the model evidence. A knowledge graph gives the model a map.

The best systems will not be "RAG systems" or "graph systems." They will be context systems. They will know when to retrieve text, when to traverse structure, when to call a tool, and when to ask a human for confirmation. That is the direction enterprise AI needs to move: not bigger prompts, but better representations of what the business actually knows.
